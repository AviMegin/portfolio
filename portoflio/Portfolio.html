Making off

For the creation of my portfolio, made a project plan with all the DOTS framework methods I was going to use .The journey began with an in-depth competitive analysis, where I delved into the portfolios of industry leaders, seeking inspiration while identifying key trends and design elements that resonate with both aesthetic appeal and functionality.

To ensure my portfolio stands out in the digital landscape, I conducted an exhaustive analysis of available products within the same domain. This process involved dissecting various portfolio styles, noting the strengths and weaknesses of each, and extracting valuable insights to inform my unique approach.
Embracing a user-centric philosophy, I initiated the design phase by developing a prototype that embodied my personal style preferences. This phase allowed me to channel my creativity into a tangible visual representation, ensuring that every aspect of the portfolio reflected my individuality.

During the search for free-to-use images, I stumbled upon captivating black and white visuals that sparked a significant shift in the website's aesthetic. Embracing this change, I adapted my portfolio to align with the newfound theme, infusing the site with a timeless and elegant essence.

Testing

The journey didn't stop at the prototype; it extended into the realm of A/B testing. By presenting subtle variations of the design to a select audience, I gauged their preferences, interactions, and overall user experience. This iterative process was instrumental in refining the design, prioritizing user satisfaction, and fine-tuning elements for optimal engagement.

The user testing phase was a crucial milestone, where real-world users provided invaluable feedback. Their insights were integrated, leading to a portfolio that not only mirrors my personal style but also caters to the preferences and expectations of the target audience.

One distinctive feature of my portfolio is the commitment to a non-scrolling website. This design choice was deliberate, aimed at breaking away from the conventional scrolling experience. The intent is to immerse visitors in a seamless, story-like navigation, eliminating the need for lengthy scrolls through monotonous text. The non-scrolling design creates an illusion, inviting users to explore different sections effortlessly, each unveiling a unique facet of my skills, projects, and personality. I also found that most people dont actually read the whole text, they usually stick with the titles and maybe th first sentence, so making important points bold or italic is great convince to the reader, since now they can glaze over the text with ease, still getting all the important points

In essence, my portfolio is not just a collection of works; it's a thoughtfully curated digital experience that invites visitors to engage, explore, and connect with my professional journey in a visually captivating and non-traditional manner.

Website lay-out

Exploring Styles: Going for a Magazine Vibe

When I started designing my website, I aimed for a cool magazine look. Picture flipping through colorful pages online. But, after checking with users, it turned out it wasn't so practical. IF everytime you wanted to see more content, you had to go through a slow page flipping animation, it would certainly become annoying

Trying Something New: Book Vibes with Sideways Scrolling

I thought, what if the site felt like reading a book, scrolling sideways? It seemed interesting, but when people tried it, it got confusing. It didn't work as well as I hoped. I still wanted to maintain the effect of "not a boring long text website"

Learning and Revisiting: Back to Parallax Scrolling with a Newspaper Twist

I took a step back and learned from the experiments. Going back to my first idea of parallax scrolling , I added a touch of a traditional newspaper layout. This time, it felt just right—keeping things interesting but also easy to use.In the end, my website got its groove with a mix of parallax scrolling and a regular newspaper layout. This way, it's easy for people to click around while still looking good. Learning from mistakes and listening to users helped me get there, making the site both cool and user-friendly.

Conclusion

In wrapping up my portfolio creation journey, I wanted to share how I carefully blended style with substance. Starting with a deep dive into what the cool kids in the industry were doing (competitive analysis), I picked up on trends that not only looked good but also worked well.

Next, I wanted my portfolio to be more than just pretty pictures . So, I kicked off the design phase by creating a prototype that screamed "me". It was like giving my creativity a visual form. But guess what? It didn't end there. I stumbled upon some amazing black and white visuals while hunting for free-to-use images. It sparked a lightbulb moment, and suddenly my portfolio had this timeless and elegant vibe.

Now, let's talk testing. I didn't just sit back and hope for the best. I put my designs to the test , literally. A/B testing became my best friend. I threw different versions at a select group, watched how they interacted, and tweaked things based on their feedback. It was like having a conversation with my future visitors without actually talking.

Real-world users were the real MVPs. Their insights were gold. I listened, learned, and made my portfolio not just about me but about what people wanted to see. And oh, I ditched the regular scrolling thing. Who needs that when you can have an immersive, story-like journey through different sections? Finally, let's talk about the layout evolution. I started with a dream of a cool magazine look, tried making it feel like reading a book (didn't work), but eventually found the sweet spot – a mix of parallax scrolling and a regular newspaper layout. It looks good, and people can easily click around. Learning from mistakes and listening to users made all the difference.

So, in a nutshell, my portfolio is not just a collection of my works. It's a digital adventure where visitors can engage, explore, and connect with my professional journey in a way that's both cool and user-friendly. And all of this was possible by combining interactive and aesthetic elements, sprinkled with a touch of AI magic.

Research questions & Dot framework
1. Research question
What are the key principles of interactive design that contribute to a positive user experience on a website?

Answer:

Key principles of interactive design for a positive user experience include starting with a user-centric focus, allowing aesthetic adaptability, conducting iterative testing based on real-world feedback, making deliberate design choices, exploring layouts, creating a dynamic narrative, and seamlessly blending interactive and aesthetic elements.

2. Research question
In what ways can AI technologies be effectively incorporated into web design to improve user interactions and content delivery?

Answer:

While AI technologies can enhance web design in various ways, it's important to note that they are not always required. If opting not to use AI, web designers can still achieve effective user interactions and content delivery

3. Research question
What is the limit between fun and annoying when it comes to usability of a website

Answer:

The line between enjoyable and irritating on a website depends on how much interactive stuff makes the site more interesting without making it hard or annoying for users, considering what users like and making sure it doesn't slow things down or feel out of place.
